## The problem

Clinical teams receive information from many directions: new evidence, product
updates, reimbursement details, training dates, and internal announcements.
When all of that arrives as dense text, the most important message can disappear
before someone reaches the second paragraph.

I created a sample issue of the **Clinical Courier** to explore how a recurring
healthcare newsletter could make complex information easier to scan without
making it feel less credible.

<Sidenote n={1}>This was an independent portfolio concept created after learning
about the newsletter workflow. It was not commissioned or published by
Kerecis.</Sidenote>

## The move

I designed the newsletter as a repeatable editorial system rather than a single
poster. Every section has a specific job, a predictable place, and a different
level of visual emphasis.

1. **Number of the Week** leads with one memorable data point.
2. **Healing Edit** explains why the finding matters in plain language.
3. **Calendar** turns upcoming training and events into an actionable checklist.

<Callout kind="method">
The design principle was simple: someone should understand the issue's main
message in thirty seconds, then be able to read deeper without leaving the same
visual hierarchy.
</Callout>

## From evidence to visual

For the sample issue, I translated a 465-patient registry finding into a simple
comparison: an average length of stay of **24.2 days** for patients treated with
intact fish-skin grafts versus **33.5 days** for the comparison group.

The large numbers create the entry point, while the supporting copy preserves
the context: the population, comparison, adjusted analysis, and limits of what
the result can claim. The goal was not to make the evidence louder. It was to
make it easier to understand correctly.

<Callout kind="finding">
Healthcare communication becomes more useful when design separates the finding,
the interpretation, and the required action instead of presenting them as one
block of copy.
</Callout>

## How I worked

I began by mapping the information architecture before styling the page. I
identified what readers needed to notice immediately, what they could explore
second, and what should become an action item.

Then I built the visual system in Figma using reusable section headers, data
cards, editorial copy blocks, and calendar rows. I also explored an HTML version
to test how the design could translate into a responsive email rather than
remaining only a static mockup.

## What I learned

A clinical newsletter is both a design problem and an operations problem. The
layout has to be attractive, but it also has to survive a weekly production
cycle with new evidence, changing dates, compliance review, and multiple
contributors.

The strongest design decisions were the ones that reduced future work: fixed
content modules, consistent source placement, reusable data visuals, and clear
space for calls to action.

## Where it goes next

The next version would add a formal source-note pattern, mobile email testing,
accessibility checks, and a lightweight content-intake template so contributors
can submit evidence and calendar updates in a consistent format.
