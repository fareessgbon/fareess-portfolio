## The problem

Clinical teams receive information from many directions: new evidence, product
updates, coverage details, training dates, and internal announcements. When all
of that arrives as dense text, the most important message can disappear before
someone reaches the second paragraph.

I created a sample issue of the **Clinical Courier** to explore how a recurring
healthcare newsletter could make complex information easier to scan without
making it feel less credible.

<Sidenote n={1}>
This was an independent portfolio concept created after learning about the
newsletter workflow. It was not commissioned, approved, or published by
Kerecis.
</Sidenote>

## The final newsletter

The completed one-page newsletter combines a clinical finding, supporting
evidence, company updates, and upcoming event dates within one consistent
editorial system.

## The move

I designed the newsletter as a repeatable editorial system rather than a
single poster. Every section has a specific job, a predictable place, and a
different level of visual emphasis.

1. **Number of the Week** leads with one memorable data point.
2. **The Healing Edit** explains the evidence and why the finding matters.
3. **Around the Company** presents shorter coverage and product updates.
4. **On the Calendar** turns upcoming meetings and events into actionable information.

<Callout kind="method">
The design principle was simple: someone should understand the issue's main
message in thirty seconds, then be able to read deeper without leaving the same
visual hierarchy.
</Callout>

## From evidence to visual

For the sample issue, I translated a 465-patient registry finding into a simple
comparison: an average length of stay of **24.2 days** for patients treated with
intact fish-skin grafts versus **33.5 days** for the comparison group.

I presented the **9.3-day difference** as the main number while keeping both
original values visible in the chart.

The supporting copy preserved important context about the patient population,
comparison group, adjusted analysis, graft loss, safety outcomes, and broader
sensitivity analysis.

<Callout kind="finding">
Healthcare communication becomes more useful when design separates the finding,
the interpretation, and the required action instead of presenting everything as
one block of copy.
</Callout>

## How I worked

I began by mapping the information architecture before styling the page. I
identified what readers needed to notice immediately, what they could explore
second, and what should become an action item.

I then built the visual system in Figma using reusable section headers, data
cards, editorial copy blocks, image modules, and calendar rows.

I also considered the recurring workflow needed to produce a new issue:

- Collecting clinical and company updates
- Reviewing evidence and source information
- Confirming calendar dates
- Checking the layout for consistency
- Completing clinical and compliance review
- Preparing the final newsletter for distribution

I also explored an HTML version to test how the design could translate into a
responsive email rather than remaining only a static mockup.

## What I learned

A clinical newsletter is both a design problem and an operations problem. The
layout has to be engaging, but it must also support a weekly production cycle
with new evidence, changing dates, compliance review, and multiple contributors.

The strongest design decisions were the ones that reduced future work: fixed
content modules, consistent source placement, reusable data visuals, and clear
space for calls to action.

## Where it goes next

The next version would add a formal source-note pattern, mobile email testing,
accessibility checks, and a lightweight content-intake template so contributors
can submit clinical evidence, company updates, and event dates in a consistent
format.
