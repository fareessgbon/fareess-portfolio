## The short version

Hush Jewels started as a design project and became a business the moment the
first stranger bought something. From then on, every decision had a number
attached to it, and the difference between a good month and a bad one was
almost always whether I had read those numbers honestly.

<Sidenote n={1}>This is the project that taught me what operations actually
means: the unglamorous system that decides whether the glamorous thing
survives.</Sidenote>

## The three systems that ran it

**Audience analysis.** Demographic and engagement data from Instagram and
Shopify decided who each campaign spoke to. Campaigns designed for a specific
segment consistently outperformed broadcast posts, so broadcast posts stopped.

**Campaign design.** Everything was graphics-led and produced in-house. Design
and analytics were never separate jobs. The analysis chose the audience, and
the design was built for that audience alone.

**The retail partnership.** Going from online-only into a physical store meant
wholesale pricing, stock tracking, a second set of records, and negotiating
shelf position with sales data in hand.

<Callout kind="finding">
The pattern that held for four years: the brand grew when decisions followed
the record, and stalled when they followed enthusiasm. The record wins.
</Callout>

## The Vogue feature

Being selected for British Vogue's "Jewelry Designer Profile" was the brand's
most visible moment, and internally its least representative one. The feature
took a morning. The system that made the brand worth featuring took four years
of careful records, and that system is what this case study is about.

## Why it's here

Hush Jewels is the reason this site has its one rule, that every number is
real. Small businesses run on self-reported metrics, and I learned to question
even my own favorites. Where I can't state a measurement source, I say so
plainly. That habit transfers directly to health data, where it is not a
preference but a requirement.
